Adaptive Trust-Based Edge Decision for IIoT Security: A PALASIK Case Paper

Author: David Rinaldi · Working Paper (2026) · Category: Edge Security, IIoT, Trust-Aware Policy

Abstract

This paper formalizes the PALASIK design as a reusable model for adaptive, low-latency security decisions in constrained edge environments. The core contribution is an operationally oriented framework that combines trust score inference, policy state machines, and audit-preserving execution, while maintaining local determinism under unstable network conditions. The practical result is a reduction in decision ambiguity and faster incident recoveries in edge nodes that are required to continue operating during partial failure conditions.

Research Problem

Many IIoT deployments are forced to choose between two risky extremes: strict cloud-dependent policy evaluation (high latency and fragile dependency) or permissive local fallback rules (security gap risk). PALASIK addresses this by enabling adaptive local enforcement that can degrade securely when confidence is uncertain.

Research Questions

  1. Can local trust scoring maintain acceptable security decision quality without constant full-cloud validation?
  2. Can policy latency remain within practical edge constraints under traffic burst and unstable connectivity?
  3. Do structured decision logs improve root-cause traceability for both operators and researchers?

Method

This study adopts a case-study design with reproducible implementation traces:

  • System under study: PALASIK edge runtime integrated through DEMIT.
  • Design pattern: trust score generation from multiple signals and policy state transition with ALLOW/RESTRICT/DENY/QUARANTINE actions.
  • Evaluation approach: scenario-based comparison between fixed rule sets and adaptive score-based decisions.
  • Data scope: event timing, decision distribution, fallback triggers, policy outcomes, and recovery intervals.

Measurement Framework

For stronger repeatability this paper uses a minimal standardized log schema:

{
  "ts": "...",
  "event_id": "uuid",
  "node_id": "edge-node-01",
  "signal": "trust_score|policy_state|network_signal",
  "trust_score": 0.84,
  "policy_action": "ALLOW",
  "reason_code": "trusted_device_normalized",
  "latency_ms": 118,
  "recovery_time_ms": 220,
  "resource_cpu": 0.39,
  "resource_mem": 0.71
}

Findings

Based on operational records and iterative tests, three empirical outcomes are emphasized:

  • Decision output becomes more stable during burst events when adaptive trust thresholds are applied instead of fixed static policy checks.
  • Fallback mode prevents full shutdown behavior in unstable conditions by preserving actionable decisions under constrained evidence.
  • Auditability improves through reason-coded logging that remains coherent across repeated policy evaluation cycles.

Discussion

The contribution is not a generic theoretical model; it is an implementation-informed model. The practical implication for edge teams is that trust-aware decisions should be designed as a continuity mechanism, not only an optimization technique. This becomes especially relevant where intermittent links, noisy sensor states, and mixed protocol quality are common.

Validity and Limitations

The current evidence is strongest for early deployment conditions and moderate traffic load. External threats from large-scale adversarial simulation and long-horizon drift of trust signals need additional experiments. Replication in industrial partner environments is recommended before claiming universal thresholds.

Research Contribution

PALASIK case evidence can be reused as:

  • Baseline for adaptive policy engines in IIoT edge controllers.
  • Comparative model for decision latency and recoverability in degraded network mode.
  • Template for audit-oriented security governance in low-latency environments.
